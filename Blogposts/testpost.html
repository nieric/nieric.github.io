A thought that has occured to me is how one can introduce students to more "grown up" physics, and how early that can be done. Sure, Netwons's equations are fine and dandy, but papers in (theoretical) physics will mainly talk about Lagranigans and Hamiltonians. Objects to which we have an association in classical physics alos makes an appearance, but may have nothing to do with the physics from which it takes its name; eg. a (conserved) current. But what exactly are the objects? Let's dive in.

Let's start off with the Lagrangian; $$\mathcal{L} = T - V$$ I certainly was a bit confused about this object the first time I saw it - the Hamiltonians $$H = T + V$$ being the total energy - kinetic plus potential - seems reasonable. What coulld possibly be encoded in the difference between the two types of energy?

Let's start from the beginning. A very elegant idea is Hamilton's principle; a physical object, say a particle, will follow the "path of least resistance".
